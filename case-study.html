Case Study Learning

Learn clinical reasoning through guided patient cases.

Patient Presentation

A 45-year-old male presents to the Emergency Department complaining of severe, crushing substernal chest pain radiating to his left shoulder, which started 45 minutes ago. He is visibly diaphoretic, nauseous, and dyspneic.

Clinical History

Patient has a history of dyslipidemia and hypertension. Strong family history of early coronary artery disease (father had an MI at age 48).

Physical Examination

Blood Pressure: 142/90 mmHg. Heart Rate: 98 bpm (sinus rhythm). Respiration: 22/min. Lungs are clear to auscultation. S1 and S2 are normal, no murmurs.

Diagnostic Investigations

12-Lead ECG demonstrates ST-segment elevations >2mm in Leads V1-V4, suggesting an acute anterior myocardial infarction. Serum troponins are ordered.

Differential Diagnosis

Acute Coronary Syndrome, Aortic Dissection, Pulmonary Embolism, Pericarditis, Esophageal Spasm.

Final Diagnosis

Acute Anterior Wall STEMI (LAD artery occlusion).

Treatment Plan

  • ✓ Administer chewable Aspirin 325 mg immediately
  • ✓ Sublingual Nitroglycerin (if blood pressure stable)
  • ✓ Emergent cardiac catheterization laboratory activation
  • ✓ Post-PCI DAPT, statin, and beta-blocker initiation

Next Step Question

What is the most critical immediate step for this patient?

Clinical Pearl

"Time is muscle." Primary PCI should be performed within 90 minutes of medical contact to minimize myocardial damage.
