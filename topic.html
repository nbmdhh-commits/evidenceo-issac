Myocardial Infarction

Cardiology Core Coursework — Pathophysiology and Clinical Progression.

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

Myocardial Infarction (MI), colloquially known as a heart attack, is the irreversible necrosis of cardiac muscle secondary to prolonged ischemia. This is most frequently caused by the sudden rupture of an unstable coronary atherosclerotic plaque, triggering platelette aggregation, coagulation cascade activation, and acute thrombotic occlusion of the coronary artery vessel.

Cellular injury begins within 20 minutes of complete occlusion. If perfusion is not restored, the necrotic area expands from the subendocardium outwards, potentially becoming transmural (involving the full thickness of the ventricular wall).

Clinical Stages & Diagnostics
Anatomy & Pathophysiology Detail +
Coronary perfusion is supplied by the Left Anterior Descending (LAD) artery, Left Circumflex (LCx), and Right Coronary Artery (RCA). Blockages in specific vessels lead to regional ischemic damages: LAD causes anterior infarcts; LCx causes lateral; RCA causes inferior infarctions.
Clinical Presentation & Symptoms +
Classical presentation includes crushing, substernal chest pressure (Levine's sign) radiating to the left arm, neck, or jaw. Commonly accompanied by diaphoresis, dyspnea, nausea, and vomiting. Diabetic patients, females, and elderly populations may present atypically (silent MIs).
Diagnostic Guidelines & Biomarkers +
12-Lead ECG must be performed and reviewed within 10 minutes of arrival. STEMI requires immediate emergency reperfusion. NSTEMI is diagnosed in the absence of persistent ST elevations but with positive cardiac biomarkers (specifically Troponins I or T).
Immediate & Secondary Management +
Immediate management involves chewed Aspirin, sublingual Nitroglycerin, Heparin anticoagulation, and emergency catheterization (PCI) or thrombolytic therapy if PCI is unavailable. Secondary prevention includes Beta Blockers, ACE inhibitors, high-intensity statins, and dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT).

High-Yield Outcomes

  • ✓ Differentiate STEMI vs. NSTEMI findings
  • ✓ Memorize the emergency 10-minute ECG window
  • ✓ Understand RV infarct preload dependency

Clinical Pearl

In patients presenting with inferior STEMI (Lead II, III, aVF elevations), always obtain a right-sided ECG (V4R). Right ventricular infarctions are highly preload-dependent; administration of vasodilators like Nitroglycerin can cause critical circulatory collapse.
